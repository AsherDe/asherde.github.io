何为统战价值

数学公式：拉拢收益－欺压收益 =统战价值

水浒传的例子

三打祝家庄后，扈三娘家破人亡：庄子被屠，父母被杀，自己被擒上梁山，哥哥也下落不明。

无依无靠的她，被迫听从宋江安排，嫁给手下败将王矮虎。

扈三娘长得貌美如花，又是富家千金，王英却形同侏儒，人品低劣，她对这件婚事很不满意，却无法抗拒，只能自我安慰：自古赖汉娶好妻，是我是命苦罢了！

她原本以为自己命苦，是因为梁山好汉不近女色，不懂美丑，自己恰好撞上了。

但大嘴巴李逵，某日醉酒后，却将宋江为求招安，对李师师如何伏低做小，投其所好，百般讨好等事说了出来。

扈三娘这才惊觉：原来宋江也知道女子喜好，也能辨别美丑，也会讲道理，只不过自己是俘虏，是梁山好汉的一员，是宋大哥的手下，没资格让他讲道理罢了。

“看来要让宋大哥讲道理，一定不能太听话，否则就没有收买价值呢”，扈三娘心想。

案例分析

在这个案例中，减数为善待三娘，被减数为得罪三娘，差就是三娘的统战价值。

显然，在宋大哥看来，得罪三娘的收益，远高于善待三娘的收益，所以三娘的统战价值为负。

但李师师就不是这个算法，宋大哥招安还指望李大美女吹枕边风呢，所以拉拢李师师的收益，远大于得罪李师师的收益，所以李师师的统战价值为正。

作为数学公式，统战价值可能为正，也有可能为负，还有可能为零，主要取决于减数和被减数。

及物和不及物

Posted on 2023-10-13 Edited on 2023-11-20

以下文章由我的挚友，一位有潜力的语言学家投稿。你可以通过邮箱联系他。

及物和不及物：你被动地感知整个视野，主动地聚焦一个点（中文）
本文基于原版 “Transitive and Intransitive: You perceive your whole view passively and focus on a pinpoint actively”由Bing Chat Copilot翻译而成，并根据中文表达习惯进行了少量调整及修改

介绍

我相信很多非英语母语人士或者不说日耳曼语系的人（我会在最后通过分类来解释我的发现）在使用介词的方面上，一直被及物和不及物动词的区别困扰着。我们看到字典条目前有两种标记，分别是“带宾语”和“无宾语”，它们分别对应着这两种动词。但是我们如何根据它们的特性来分类呢？ 在这篇文章中，我将探讨英语、日语（其实也是中文，因为这里完全使用了汉字）中及物和不及物动词的区别。及物动词是可以带直接宾语的动词，而不及物动词是不能带直接宾语的动词。这种区别对于介词的使用、主语的角色、主动性和主观性的表达都有影响。我将比较和对比这些语言如何处理这种区别，以及它揭示了它们的什么语法和语义特征。

一个人的主动性和行为本身的范围之间的联系

日语中的例子

我首先调查了日语，因为它有一种清晰而一致的标记及物和不及物动词的方式。在日语中，及物动词叫做他動詞（tadoushi），字面意思是“他移动动词”或“他推进动词”，而不及物动词叫做自動詞（jidoushi），字面意思是“自移动动词”或“自推进动词”。这些术语表明了动词是否表达了一种影响另一个实体的行为。例如，“ドアが開いた”（doa ga aita）意思是“门开了”，其中门既是行为的主体又是行为的施事者，“ドアを開けた”（doa o aketa）意思是“我打开了门”，其中门是行为的对象，而我是行为的施事者。这种区别通过使用不同的动词形式（開いた vs 開けた）和不同的助词（が vs を）来标记。 很容易推理出主语的角色是决定性的。而且如果被作用于的对象在形式上不存在，那么被作用于的东西也就是发出动作的东西。
让我们回到及物和不及物的含义和定义。
及物的含义：“能够跨越”
及物的定义：“能够带一个直接宾语（明示或暗示）”
对于不及物，反之亦然。
我想知道它们之间的联系以及它们交织在一起的内在逻辑。

回到英语中的每个部分

但是日语不是英语，它们甚至没有任何亲缘关系。然后我把一些简单句子中的每个单词拆开来。 “I saw him.” “I looked at him.” 然后我发现当我使用不及物动词时，我需要一个介词来表示行为的状态，但是为什么我使用及物动词时就不需要呢？然后我查了这两个单词的字典定义，以下是它们最基本的定义。
See (带宾语): “用眼睛去感受，在视觉上察觉”
Look (无宾语): “将凝视投向某人或某物或指定的方向”和“有着……的外观或给人存在……的印象”
我把这些句子完整地和每个单词分开地读了一遍，把它们和我的认知联系起来。很容易看出它们之间的区别。“I saw him”意思是我“用眼睛感知他”，这是被动接受。“I looked at him”意思是我“把目光投向他”，这是主动参与。
当我投射我的目光时，只有一束光线发出，我需要用一个介词来指明我的对象，所以这种词是“不及物的”，它不能跨越对象，所以我们需要一个介词来指定。但是为什么及物动词不需要呢？因为你可以感知你视野中的一切，所以你不需要一些指示来显示方向。你的视野中的一切面对着你，但你的目光只能聚焦在一个特定的微小部分。

更多例子来证实和说明它们之间的相互关系

还有一些常见的动词组可以说明及物和不及物动词之间的区别，比如“reach”和“arrive”。让我把它们的核心定义贴出来，以便仔细研究。
Arrive (无宾语): “到达旅程或旅程的一个阶段的终点”
Reach (带宾语): “arrive at; 到达和……一样远”
还有另一个意思是Reach (无宾语，带方位状语): “伸出一只手臂朝着指定的方向，以便触摸或抓住某物”
根据日语的规则，我发现关键是“行为的载体”是否存在决定了词的性质。“reach (无宾语)”的最基本定义是由其手段来指定的。起初，它需要手臂作为工具，并使用许多介词来描述获取然后达到某物的运动。我假设后来它开始体现到达，并省略了介词，因为当它指的是到达某个地方时，它是一个线性和直接的概念，就像我提出的视线投射一样。然后它的外观因为定义“直接指向对象与否”而改变了，但它的内涵仍然是统一的。我认为“arrive”也有一个根据其逻辑演变的过程。组成它部分的意思是“到达岸边”，然后它被用来描述到达某处，但一开始它的范围是精确的，需要介词来指出目的地。至于“see”和“look”的定义，你用眼睛作为工具“看”某物，你“看着”某物。“look”表示你“把目光投向”，“at”表示目的地。前者是由工具“眼睛”操作的，后者是由指向你行为“目光”的和用“介词”指明对象操作的，这在逻辑上不是由某种工具推动的。因此，这是对标题论点的一个强有力的证明，关于行为本身主动性和你视觉之间的联系。

视觉相关词语之间的类比

到目前为止，我们可以解决所有与视觉相关的动词类别。如果我主动地投射我的目光，I“look at” “gaze at” “glance at”。如果我从我的视野中接收信息，I“see” “perceive” “glimpse” (由“glimmer”衍生)。至于介词，就我个人而言，“at”表示行为的目标，“to”表示路线。所以很容易理解为什么“shout at”和“shout to”之间有区别。

一些补充说明

您可能会注意到，“see”有一个从属条目，显示它也可以作为一个不及物动词来指定运动的路线。不及物动词和及物动词的区别是，动词是否通过一个精确而有限的方向指引来连接宾语。例如，在我的文章中所提到的一个是“reach”，“reach”是及物动词时，它表示广泛的到达。但是当它表示手的运动时，我们需要指出一个微小部分内的特定范围。所以这里对于“reach”和“see”来说，它们都是一个条件性的补充，因为它们有一种被动的广泛接受的意义，为使用介词来主动地定位提供了广阔的范围。——至于一个本质上的不及物动词，介词是指定特定方向的必要指示，因为动词只能执行一个动作，而不能将其引导到一个终点。它的主动性决定了它的有限范围，因为我们的注意力也是有限的。
我对罗曼语系有一些了解，基于我B1的西班牙语水平和略懂一些的法语，作为一个自称的语言学家，我学习了一些关于日耳曼语系的结构和特性（但我不懂德语或荷兰语，除了一些英语中的同源词）。我发现当涉及到不及物动词的使用时，西日耳曼语系的语言，如英语、德语和荷兰语，倾向于使用一些特定的介词来指出运动的状态。日耳曼语系的语言也有一个特点，就是动词在变化时总是改变其元音来改变其时态或词性。至于罗曼语系的语言，如西班牙语和法语，它们并不区分它们。有时，西班牙语中的“a”（像“to”，表示归属或过渡，给我一种在途中的感觉）充当了类似的角色，但有着不同的逻辑。当我的西班牙语水平提高后，我会尝试解释它。

最后……结束了！

作为一个自称的语言学家，我的理论仅基于我的个人观察和推理的视角，它可能不适用于所有的语言或情况。可能还有其他的因素或维度影响着及物和不及物动词之间的区别，比如体态、情态、语态或价值。也可能有不同的方式来解释或说明我呈现的数据。例如，有人可能会认为及物和不及物动词之间的区别与行为和视觉的主动性无关，而是与论元的语义角色或主题关系有关，比如施事者、受事者、主题、目标、来源等。这些是我理论可能面临的一些可能的挑战或批评，我欢迎其他的语言学家或语言学习者进一步讨论和反馈。 我希望这篇短文可以帮助你扩展你的认知，如果你有任何问题或发现任何瑕疵，请通过我的电子邮箱dingzitchui@outlook.com联系我。

Transitive/and/Intransitive

Posted on 2023-10-12 Edited on 2023-10-31

The following paper was submitted by my dear friend, a potential linguist. You can reach him by e-mail.

Transitive and Intransitive: You perceive your whole view passively and focus on a pinpoint actively

Introduction

I believe that many non-English speakers or people who don’t speak Germanic languages (I’ll explain my discovery through sorting at the end) have been plagued by the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs for a long time in terms of the use of prepositions. We see there are two kinds of marks ahead of the dictionary entries presented as “with object” and “no object” respectively, which correspond to them separately. But how can we sort them based on their properties?
In this essay, I will explore the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs in English and Japanese (it’s also Chinese indeed because of the complete use of Chinese characters here). Transitive verbs are verbs that can take a direct object, while intransitive verbs are verbs that cannot. This distinction has implications for the use of prepositions, the role of the subject, and the expression of agency and initiative. I will compare and contrast how these languages handle this distinction and what it reveals about their grammatical and semantic features.

The connection between one’s initiative and the spectrum of the action itself

The cases in Japanese

I will examine them first in Japanese, which has a clear and consistent way of marking transitive and intransitive verbs. In Japanese, transitive verbs are called 他動詞 (tadoushi), which means “other-moving verb” or “other-propulsive verb”, while intransitive verbs are called 自動詞 (jidoushi), which means “self-moving verb” or “self-propulsive verb”. These terms indicate whether the verb expresses an action that affects another entity or not. For example, ドアが開いた (doa ga aita) means “The door opened”, where the door is both the subject and the agent of the action, while ドアを開けた (doa o aketa) means “I opened the door”, where the door is the object and I am the agent of the action. The difference is marked by the use of different verb forms (開いた vs 開けた) and different particles (が vs を).
It’s clear and easy to reason that the role of the subject is decisive. And if the object that’s acted upon is absent in form, something that’s acted upon is also the thing that acts.
Let’s go back to the meanings and definitions of transitive and intransitive.
The meaning of transitive: “be able to go across”
The definition of transitive: “able to take a direct object (expressed or implied)”
Vice versa for the intransitive.
And I wondered about the connection between them and their intertwined inner logic.

Back to each part of English

But Japanese is not English, they are not even relatives in any degree. Then I took apart each word in some easy sentences.
“I saw him.” “I looked at him.”
Then I discovered that I needed a preposition to indicate the state of the action when I used the intransitive, but why didn’t I need one for a transitive verb? Then I looked up the definitions of these two words from a dictionary and here are their most basic definitions.
See (with object): “perceive with the eyes; discern visually”
Look (no object): “direct one’s gaze toward someone or something or in a specified direction” and “have the appearance or give the impression of being”
I read these sentences totally and each word separately to connect them to my cognition. It’s easy to tell the difference between them. “I saw him” means I “perceived him with my eyes”, which is passive reception. “I looked at him” means I “directed my gaze” to him, which is active participation.
The last question was why the preposition is necessary only for an intransitive verb. Then I raised my head, perceived everything in my view, and gazed at some things. And I finally understood through incorporating the initiative of actions in Japanese and the vision of humans in English. When I cast my eyes there’s only a beam emitted and I need to use a preposition to pinpoint my object, so this kind of word is “intransitive”, it’s not able to go across the object so we need a preposition to specify. But why doesn’t the transitive verb need one? Because you can perceive everything within your sight and you don’t need some indication to show the direction. Everything in your sight faces you but you can only focus on a specific tiny part.

More examples of their mutual corroboration and correlation

There are still some common groups of verbs that illustrate the difference between transitive and intransitive, such as “reach” and “arrive”. Let me post their core definitions to scrutinize them.
Arrive (no object): “reach a place at the end of a journey or a stage in a journey”
Reach (with object): “arrive at; get as far as”
Another meaning of Reach (no object, with adverbial of direction): “stretch out an arm in a specified direction in order to touch or grasp something”
Based on the regulation in Japanese, I found that the gist is that whether the “carrier of action” exists or not determines the property of the word. The most basic definition of “reach (no object)” is specified by its means. At first, it entails arms as the tool and uses many prepositions to describe the movement to get and then attain something. I assume then it started to embody the arrival and omit the prepositions because it’s a linear and direct concept when it refers to getting to someplace like the casting of sight I’ve put forward. Then its appearance changed due to the definition “directly refer to the object or not”, but its connotation is still uniform. I think there is also an evolution according to its logic for “arrive”. The meaning of its elements is “get to the shore” then it has been used to describe reaching somewhere but at the beginning, its range is precise and requires the preposition to point out the destination. As for the definitions of “see” and “look”, you “see” with your eyes as a tool and you “look at” something. “Look” indicates your “direct your gaze” and “at” indicates the destination. The former is operated by the tool “eyes” and the latter is operated by directing your action “gaze” and pinpointing the object by “preposition”, which is not propelled by some tool logically. As a result, it’s a robust demonstration of the argument for the title, about the connection between the initiative of the action itself and your vision.

The analogy of vision-related words

So far, we can resolve the categories of all verbs associated with your vision. If I cast my eyes actively, I “look at” “gaze at” and “glance at”. If I receive messages from my view, I “see” “perceive” and “glimpse” (derived from the word “glimmer”). As for the prepositions, just from my perspective, “at” implies the destination of an action, and “to” implies the route. So it’s easy to understand why there is a difference between “shout at” and “shout to”.

Some supplementary explanations

You might notice that there’s a subordinate entry for “see” that shows that it can also serve as an intransitive verb to specify the route of the movement. The distinction between intransitive and transitive verbs is whether the verb connects itself to the object by a precise and limited guide in direction or not. For example, the one I referred to in my essay is “reach”, “reach” is transitive when it indicates broad arrival. But when it denotes the movement of the hand, we need to pinpoint a specific range within a tiny part. So it’s indeed a conditional supplement for both “reach” and “see” here because they have a meaning of passive broad reception that provides the broad scope for using a preposition to pinpoint actively. — As for an essentially intransitive verb, a preposition is a necessary indicator of a specific direction due to the verb’s property of only being able to perform an action instead of leading it to an end. Its initiative determines its limited range, because our attention is also limited.
I know some about Romance languages based on my Spanish about B1 level and some French, and as a self-proclaimed linguist, I learned a little about Germanic languages about their structure and properties (but I don’t know German or Dutch except some of their cognates in English). I discovered that when it comes to the use of intransitive verbs, West Germanic languages like English, German, and Dutch tend to use some specific prepositions to point out the state of movement. The Germanic languages also have a feature that the verb always alters its vowel when conjugating to change its tense or part of speech. And as for Romance languages like Spanish and French, they don’t distinguish them. Sometimes, the word “a” in Spanish (like “to”, indicating belonging or transition giving me the sense of being on route) serves as a similar role but with a different logic. When my Spanish improves, I’ll try to explain it.

Finally… The end!

There may be other factors or dimensions that influence the distinction between transitive and intransitive verbs, such as aspect, modality, voice, or valency. There may also be different ways of interpreting or explaining the data that I have presented. For example, one could argue that the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs is not related to the initiative of action and vision, but rather to the semantic roles or thematic relations of the arguments, such as agent, patient, theme, goal, source, etc. These are some of the possible challenges or criticisms that my theory may face, and I welcome further discussion and feedback from other linguists or language learners.
