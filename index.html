主人的良苦用心

Posted on 2023-10-22

以下故事完全由我虚空编造，是写给儿童看的童话故事，然而事实证明本人没有写童话的天赋，故事漏洞百出，作者在凌晨时分所写，所有内容纯属作者痴人说梦，请不要曲解作者的原意，不要生搬硬套到其他任何地方，所有人有权利保留自己的看法，但是请相信这只是一篇童话故事。

主人与狗

庄园里有惊天大谣言在狗的圈子传开了，说是主人和狗一样，一张嘴巴俩眼睛，除了生活习惯和狗没什么区别。大多数狗都不以为意，只是当作茶余饭后谈资罢了。
庄园主人养了许多狗，狗的工作很多，但是狗的数量太多，导致大部分狗还是很闲。这些狗到晚上不睡觉，在家里四处活动甚至搞破坏，于是主人第二天领这些狗出去，扔飞盘给这群狗接，一白天的捡飞盘过去，狗果然晚上听话多了。

下人与狗

主人有时很忙，就把这项任务交给下人去做，下人扔飞盘有的狗不愿意接，下人就把食物多分给愿意接飞盘的狗，激励其他狗接飞盘。

狗与上级

本身就聪明的狗们学会了叼着盘子在不积极的狗面前炫耀，用后肢站立起来，学着人的样子扔盘子给不积极的狗，他们会说话，会用它们用犬吠的方式表示“大家都这样，就你特殊？”“你不接盘子，有的是狗接”，用激将法敦促其他狗，他们甚至组织起了各种集体表演，盘子在空中翻飞，下面的狗用尽了花样接盘子，博得主人和下人一笑。于是这部分狗获得了主人的青睐，得到了多于其他狗的食物。积极的狗被认定为“积极接盘狗”，会被领头的狗给予更优质更充足的食物。有时主人还会带着其他人来，让自己家的狗表演绝技，这让主人很有面子，甚至买来了更多的狗，组建起了马戏团————或者我们应该叫狗戏团，毕竟一群智慧的生物被同类控制的场面要有趣的多了。聪明的狗能指挥的狗数量越来越大，领头的狗甚至组织起来专门的狗去向观众要钱，让主人购买更多的狗。

狗还是主人

主任逐渐发现事态失去了控制，主人养了太多的狗，门票的收入和养狗的支出完全不对等，在经济效益考量下要将一部分狗卖掉，在此事上与领头的狗起了冲突，领头的狗顾及与其他狗的友谊不愿卖狗。于是主人想将权力收回，试图与其他聪明的狗联合将领头狗赶出庄园，而领头的狗也不是省油的灯，最终领头的狗以微弱的优势获胜。它给自己起了名字，借了主人的姓氏，而它也早已习惯了如人一般两腿走路。为了稳固他的地位，他将倒向前主人的聪明的狗及其部下一律赶出了庄园。然而还是会有闲着的狗，飞盘游戏仍在继续。于其他狗而言，领头的狗是不是狗不重要，无非另一个主人罢了

及物和不及物

Posted on 2023-10-13 Edited on 2023-10-14

以下文章由我的挚友，一位有潜力的语言学家投稿。你可以通过邮箱联系他。

及物和不及物：你被动地感知整个视野，主动地聚焦一个点（中文）

        本文完全基于原版 “Transitive and Intransitive: 
        You perceive your whole view passively and 
        focus on a pinpoint actively”由Bing Chat 
        Copilot翻译而成，并根据中文表达习惯进行了少量调整及修改

介绍

我相信很多不说英语或者不说日耳曼语系的人（我会在最后通过分类来解释我的发现）在使用介词的方面上，一直被及物和不及物动词的区别困扰着。我们看到字典条目前有两种标记，分别是“带宾语”和“无宾语”，它们对应着这两种动词。但是我们如何根据它们的特性来分类呢？ 在这篇文章中，我将探讨英语、日语（其实也是中文，因为这里完全使用了汉字）中及物和不及物动词的区别。及物动词是可以带直接宾语的动词，而不及物动词是不能带直接宾语的动词。这种区别对于介词的使用、主语的角色、主动性和主观性的表达都有影响。我将比较和对比这些语言如何处理这种区别，以及它揭示了它们的什么语法和语义特征。

一个人的主动性和行为本身的范围之间的联系

日语中的例子

我首先调查了日语，因为它有一种清晰而一致的标记及物和不及物动词的方式。在日语中，及物动词叫做他動詞（tadoushi），字面意思是“他移动动词”或“他推进动词”，而不及物动词叫做自動詞（jidoushi），字面意思是“自移动动词”或“自推进动词”。这些术语表明了动词是否表达了一种影响另一个实体的行为。例如，“ドアが開いた”（doa ga aita）意思是“门开了”，其中门既是行为的主体又是行为的施事者，“ドアを開けた”（doa o aketa）意思是“我打开了门”，其中门是行为的对象，而我是行为的施事者。这种区别通过使用不同的动词形式（開いた vs 開けた）和不同的助词（が vs を）来标记。 很容易推理出主语的角色是决定性的。而且如果被作用于的对象在形式上不存在，那么被作用于的东西也就是作用于东西。

让我们回到及物和不及物的含义和定义。
及物的含义：“能够跨越”
及物的定义：“能够带一个直接宾语（明示或暗示）”
对于不及物，反之亦然。
我想知道它们之间的联系以及它们交织在一起的内在逻辑。

回到英语中的每个部分

但是日语不是英语，它们甚至没有任何亲缘关系。然后我把一些简单句子中的每个单词拆开来。 “I saw him.” “I looked at him.” 然后我发现当我使用不及物动词时，我需要一个介词来表示行为的状态，但是为什么我使用及物动词时就不需要呢？然后我查了一下这两个单词的字典定义，这里是它们最基本的定义。See (带宾语): “用眼睛去感受，在视觉上察觉” Look (无宾语): “将凝视投向某人或某物或指定的方向”和“有着……的外观或给人存在……的印象” 我把这些句子完整地和每个单词分开地读了一遍，把它们和我的认知联系起来。很容易看出它们之间的区别。“I saw him”意思是我“用眼睛感知他”，这是被动接受。“I looked at him”意思是我“把目光投向他”，这是主动参与。
当我投射我的目光时，只有一束光线发出，我需要用一个介词来指明我的对象，所以这种词是“不及物的”，它不能跨越对象，所以我们需要一个介词来指定。但是为什么及物动词不需要呢？因为你可以感知你视野中的一切，所以你不需要一些指示来显示方向。你的视野中的一切面对着你，但你的目光只能聚焦在一个特定的微小部分。

更多例子来证实和说明它们之间的相互关系

还有一些常见的动词组可以说明及物和不及物动词之间的区别，比如“reach”和“arrive”。让我把它们的核心定义贴出来，以便仔细研究。
Arrive (无宾语): “到达旅程或旅程的一个阶段的终点”
Reach (带宾语): “arrive at; 到达和……一样远”
还有另一个意思是Reach (无宾语，带方位状语): “伸出一只手臂朝着指定的方向，以便触摸或抓住某物” 根据日语的规则，我发现关键是“行为的载体”是否存在决定了词的性质。“reach (无宾语)”的最基本定义是由其手段来指定的。起初，它需要手臂作为工具，并使用许多介词来描述获取然后达到某物的运动。我假设后来它开始体现到达，并省略了介词，因为当它指的是到达某个地方时，它是一个线性和直接的概念，就像我提出的视线投射一样。然后它的外观因为定义“直接指向对象与否”而改变了，但它的内涵仍然是统一的。我认为“arrive”也有一个根据其逻辑演变的过程。它元素的意思是“到达岸边”，然后它被用来描述到达某处，但一开始它的范围是精确的，需要介词来指出目的地。至于“see”和“look”的定义，你用眼睛作为工具“看”某物，你“看着”某物。“look”表示你“把目光投向”，“at”表示目的地。前者是由工具“眼睛”操作的，后者是由指向你行为“目光”的和用“介词”指明对象操作的，这在逻辑上不是由某种工具推动的。因此，这是对标题论点的一个强有力的证明，关于行为本身主动性和你视觉之间的联系。

视觉相关词语之间的类比

到目前为止，我们可以解决所有与视觉相关的动词类别。如果我主动地投射我的目光，我“看着” “凝视着” “扫了一眼”。如果我从我的视野中接收信息，我“看见” “感知” “一瞥”（从单词“微光”衍生出来）。至于介词，就我个人而言，“at”表示行为的目标，“to”表示路线。所以很容易理解为什么“shout at”和“shout to”之间有区别。

一些补充说明

我对罗曼语系有一些了解，基于我B1的西班牙语水平和略懂一些的法语，作为一个自称的语言学家，我学习了一些关于日耳曼语系的结构和特性（但我不懂德语或荷兰语，除了一些英语中的同源词）。我发现当涉及到不及物动词的使用时，西日耳曼语系的语言，如英语、德语和荷兰语，倾向于使用一些特定的介词来指出运动的状态。日耳曼语系的语言也有一个特点，就是动词在变化时总是改变其元音来改变其时态或词性。至于罗曼语系的语言，如西班牙语和法语，它们并不区分它们。有时，西班牙语中的“a”（像“to”，表示归属或过渡，给我一种在途中的感觉）起到了类似的功能，但有着不同的逻辑。当我的西班牙语水平提高后，我会尝试解释它。

最后……结束了！

作为一个自称的语言学家，我的理论仅基于我的个人观察和推理的视角，它可能不适用于所有的语言或情况。可能还有其他的因素或维度影响着及物和不及物动词之间的区别，比如体态、情态、语态或价值。也可能有不同的方式来解释或说明我呈现的数据。例如，有人可能会认为及物和不及物动词之间的区别与行为和视觉的主动性无关，而是与论元的语义角色或主题关系有关，比如施事者、受事者、主题、目标、来源等。这些是我理论可能面临的一些可能的挑战或批评，我欢迎其他的语言学家或语言学习者进一步讨论和反馈。 我希望这次修改能帮助你改进你的语言学论文，并使它更清晰、连贯和有说服力。如果你有任何问题或需要更多帮助，请告诉我。

Transitive/and/Intransitive

Posted on 2023-10-12 Edited on 2023-10-14

The following paper was submitted by my dear friend, a potential linguist. You can reach him by e-mail.

Transitive and Intransitive: You perceive your whole view passively and focus on a pinpoint actively

Introduction

I believe that many non-English speakers or people who don’t speak Germanic languages (I’ll explain my discovery through sorting at the end) have been plagued by the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs for a long time in terms of the use of prepositions. We see there are two kinds of marks ahead of the dictionary entries presented as “with object” and “no object” respectively, which correspond to them. But how can we sort them based on their properties?
In this essay, I will explore the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs in English, Japanese (it’s also Chinese indeed because of the complete use of Chinese characters here). Transitive verbs are verbs that can take a direct object, while intransitive verbs are verbs that cannot. This distinction has implications for the use of prepositions, the role of the subject, and the expression of agency and initiative. I will compare and contrast how these languages handle this distinction and what it reveals about their grammatical and semantic features.

The connection between one’s initiative and the spectrum of the action itself

The cases in Japanese

I will examine them first in Japanese, which has a clear and consistent way of marking transitive and intransitive verbs. In Japanese, transitive verbs are called 他動詞 (tadoushi), which literally means “other-moving verb” or “other-propulsive verb”, while intransitive verbs are called 自動詞 (jidoushi), which literally means “self-moving verb” or “self-propulsive verb”. These terms indicate whether the verb expresses an action that affects another entity or not. For example, ドアが開いた (doa ga aita) means “The door opened”, where the door is both the subject and the agent of the action, while ドアを開けた (doa o aketa) means “I opened the door”, where the door is the object and I am the agent of the action. The difference is marked by the use of different verb forms (開いた vs 開けた) and different particles (が vs を).
It’s clear and easy to reason that the role of the subject is decisive. And if the object which is acted upon is absent in form, something that is acted upon is also the thing that acts.

Let’s go back to the meanings and definitions of transitive and intransitive.
The meaning of transitive: “be able to go across”
The definition of transitive: “able to take a direct object (expressed or implied)”
Vice versa for the intransitive.
And I wondered about the connection between them and their intertwined inner logic.

Back to each part of English

But Japanese is not English, they are not even relatives in any degree. Then I took apart each word in some easy sentences.
“I saw him.” “I looked at him.”
Then I discovered that I needed a preposition to indicate the state of the action when I used the intransitive, but why didn’t I need one for a transitive verb? Then I looked up the definitions of these two words from a dictionary and here are their most basic definitions. See (with object): “perceive with the eyes; discern visually” Look (no object): “direct one’s gaze toward someone or something or in a specified direction” and “have the appearance or give the impression of being” I read these sentences totally and each word separately to connect them to my cognition. It’s easy to tell the difference between them. “I saw him” means I “perceived him with my eyes”, which is passive reception. “I looked at him” means I “directed my gaze” to him, which is active participation.
The last question was why the preposition is necessary only for an intransitive verb. Then I raised my head, perceived everything in my view and gazed at some things. And I finally understood through incorporating the initiative of actions in Japanese and the vision of humans in English. When I cast my eyes there’s only a beam emitted and I need to use a preposition to pinpoint my object, so this kind of word is “intransitive”, it’s not able to go across the object so we need a preposition to specify. But why doesn’t the transitive verb need one? Because you can perceive everything within your sight so you don’t need some indication to show the direction. Everything in your sight faces you but you can only focus on a specific tiny part.

More examples of their mutual corroboration and correlation

There are still some common groups of verbs that illustrate the difference between transitive and intransitive, such as “reach” and “arrive”. Let me post their core definitions in order to scrutinize them. Arrive (no object): “reach a place at the end of a journey or a stage in a journey” Reach (with object): “arrive at; get as far as” And another meaning of Reach (no object, with adverbial of direction): “stretch out an arm in a specified direction in order to touch or grasp something” Based on the regulation in Japanese, I found that the gist is that whether the “carrier of action” exists or not determines the property of the word. The most basic definition of “reach (no object)” is specified by its means. At first, it entails arms as the tool and uses many prepositions to describe the movement to get then attain something. I assume then it started to embody the arrival and omit the prepositions because it’s a linear and direct concept when it refers to getting to some place like the casting of sight I’ve put forward. And then its appearance changed due to the definition “directly refer to the object or not”, but its connotation is still uniform. I think there is also an evolution according to its logic for “arrive”. The meaning of its elements is “get to the shore” then it has been used to describe reaching somewhere but at the beginning its range is precise and requires the preposition to point out the destination. As for the definitions of “see” and “look”, you “see” with your eyes as a tool and you “look at” something. “Look” indicates your “direct your gaze” and “at” indicates the destination. The former is operated by the tool “eyes” and the latter is operated by directing your action “gaze” and pinpointing the object by “preposition”, which is not propelled by some tool logically. As a result, it’s a robust demonstration of the argument for the title, about the connection between the initiative of the action itself and your vision.

The analogy of vision-related words

So far, we can resolve the categories of all verbs associated with your vision. If I cast my eyes actively, I “look at” “gaze at” “glance at” If I receive messages from my view, I “see” “perceive” “I glimpse” (derived from the word “glimmer”) As for the prepositions, just from my perspective, “at” implies the destination of an action and “to” implies the route. So it’s easy to understand why there is a difference between “shout at” and “shout to”.

Some supplementary explanations

I know some about Romance languages based on my Spanish about B1 level and some French, and as a self-proclaimed linguist, I learned a little about Germanic languages about their structure and properties (but I don’t know German or Dutch except some of their cognates in English). I discovered that when it comes to the use of intransitive verbs, West Germanic languages like English, German, and Dutch tend to use some specific prepositions to point out the state of movement. The Germanic languages also have a feature that the verb always alters its vowel when conjugating to change its tense or part of speech. And as for Romance languages like Spanish and French, they don’t distinguish them. Sometimes, the word “a” in Spanish (like “to”, indicating belonging or transition giving me the sense of being on route) serves as a similar function but with a different logic. When my Spanish improves, I’ll try to explain it.

Finally… The end!

As a self-proclaimed linguist, my theory is based on my personal observations and reasoning, and it may not apply to all languages or situations. There may be other factors or dimensions that influence the distinction between transitive and intransitive verbs, such as aspect, modality, voice, or valency. There may also be different ways of interpreting or explaining the data that I have presented. For example, one could argue that the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs is not related to the initiative of action and vision, but rather to the semantic roles or thematic relations of the arguments, such as agent, patient, theme, goal, source, etc. These are some of the possible challenges or criticisms that my theory may face, and I welcome further discussion and feedback from other linguists or language learners.
I hope this revision will help you improve your linguistic essay and make it more clear, coherent, and convincing. If you have any questions or need more help, please let me know.

职业生涯随想

Posted on 2023-10-09

提升专业技能

未来是AI的时代，然而我认为AI并不是一场革命，更不会完全取代人类的工作，反之他象征着人类将从重复的工作中得到解放，象征着人类生产力的进一步提升。
当我使用ChatGPT阅读一篇长文，它可以轻松分析出文章的逻辑结构，可以借助文章本身以及其他资料解释你提出的任何问题，但是我并不觉得他会具备一位合格教师所应该有的除了答疑和读PPT以外的其他专业技能。
当我使用copilot编写代码时，ai可以通过你的只言片语推断出你想要表达的逻辑结构，一瞬间智能补全几十行代码，因为这些代码都是之前有人写过的，我们学习的是前人的思考成果，AI所做不到的是我们创造的能力，这也进一步要求我们需要有更进一步的专业能力，而非简单的重复。

提高人文素养

语言是文化的大门，一方面要了解中国的历史文化，了解文学作品，历史发展，不能被功利主义所裹挟而沦为工具人，同时也有助于我们培养自己的判断力。另一方面语言是文化的大门，通过学习和使用外语能让我们看到地球另一端的世界，以他人为镜，可以完善自己，以语言为桥，可以跨过不同文化的鸿沟。各国互相难分难舍，我们的国家将会需要大量既有扎实的专业知识又有丰富的人文知识储备的人才。这也是为什么英语是我们从小需要学习的主科。当有国际视野，成为综合水平强、真正国际化的人才。

心系天下

科技的飞速发展很大程度离不开自然环境，Google，Microsoft，Amazon等公司公布了2022年的用水数据，仅微软一个月就使用了4353万升水，这些水尽管最终会回到地面，但是仍然需要很长的时间，直接造成美国爱荷华州饮用水匮乏。而且大量用水会造成一系列连锁反应，对自然产生不可预测的影响。其中很大的原因就是训练AI需要使用大量的水，平均问ChatGPT五到五十个问题就会消耗500ml水。类似的事情一直都在发生，可能对我们这一代人影响不大。着眼长远，这是全人类的问题，就如墨西哥科学家主导解决臭氧层空洞，各国领导人签署《蒙特利尔议定书》一样，地球的问题也是全人类的问题，值得每一个人关注。环境问题未来会是我们无可避免的责任。

结语

我们作为新时代的青年，应该积极关注社会发展，承担全社会乃至全人类的责任，让自己成为有用之人。

Everybody dies but not everybody lives.

生而渺小
